\begin{abstract}
Chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring is increasingly deployed as
an AI safety tool, but its reliability depends on CoT reasoning
being parametrically faithful --- causally connected to the model's
internal predictions. We investigate whether fine-tuning on
high-quality, human-preferred CoT outputs affects this parametric
faithfulness, as measured by Negative Preference Optimization
(NPO) unlearning. We find a consistent \textbf{alignment-faithfulness
paradox}: high-quality CoT fine-tuning reduces parametric
faithfulness by 56--73\% relative across two model families
(LLaMA-3-3B: 30.0\%$\to$8.0\%, $p{=}0.005$; Phi-3:
32.0\%$\to$14.0\%, $p{=}0.033$), while low-quality fine-tuning
leaves faithfulness unchanged. We identify the mechanism through
step-level analysis: non-faithful CoT steps score significantly
higher on quality metrics than faithful steps (3.61 vs.\ 3.33,
$p{=}0.008$, $N{=}940$), meaning quality filtering
preferentially selects causally-decoupled reasoning.
We validate parametric faithfulness against human step-level
annotations from REVEAL \citep{jacovi2025reveal}, finding that
steps in logically correct answers have significantly higher
$\Delta p$ than steps in incorrect answers ($+0.110$
vs.\ $-0.084$, $p{<}0.001$, $N{=}1{,}276$), while
surface-level evidence attribution shows no relationship
($\rho{=}0.04$, n.s.). These results suggest that training on
better-looking reasoning actively selects against the reasoning
steps that drive model predictions --- a direct challenge to
CoT-based AI safety monitoring.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Chain-of-Thought prompting has become a cornerstone of modern language model
deployment \citep{wei2022cot}. By conditioning models to produce intermediate
reasoning steps before answering, practitioners report improvements in
accuracy and interpretability across a wide range of tasks
\citep{kojima2022zeroshot,lightman2023prm}. The implicit assumption is that
these steps are \emph{faithful}: the model uses the expressed reasoning to
arrive at its answer. If this assumption holds, improving reasoning quality
through fine-tuning should simultaneously improve interpretability.

However, faithfulness and quality are conceptually distinct. A CoT can be
internally coherent, factually accurate, and stylistically polished while
playing no causal role in the model's prediction. Prior work has documented
exactly this dissociation: models produce plausible-sounding reasoning chains
that are post-hoc rationalizations rather than generative explanations
\citep{turpin2023faithfulness,lanham2023measuring,lyu2023faithful}. The
mechanism of faithfulness --- how reasoning steps encoded in transformer
weights causally influence output distributions --- remains poorly understood.

\citet{tutek2025faithfulness} propose a rigorous operationalization:
\emph{parametric faithfulness} measures whether surgically unlearning a CoT
step (via Negative Preference Optimization applied to FF2 layers) changes the
model's answer. This probes whether the information in the expressed reasoning
step is encoded in the same parameters that drive the final prediction. Their
main finding is that faithfulness so measured is empirically distinct from
accuracy, varies by model family and dataset, and correlates strongly with
unlearning efficacy ($r = 0.937$, $p < 0.0001$ in our reproduction).

In this work, we ask a direct follow-up: \textbf{does fine-tuning on
high-quality CoT outputs increase or decrease parametric faithfulness?} This
question has practical import. If the answer is ``increases,'' then standard
instruction-following fine-tuning on curated reasoning data is a path toward
more interpretable models. If the answer is ``decreases'' or ``null,'' then
surface quality and computational faithfulness are dissociated, and we cannot
assume that CoT quality improvements translate to faithfulness improvements.

We call the hypothesis that quality fine-tuning reduces faithfulness the
\emph{alignment-faithfulness paradox}. The mechanism we propose is as
follows: high-quality CoT fine-tuning strengthens the association between
linguistic reasoning patterns and correct answers in the model's surface
behavior, but this association may be mediated by shallow token-prediction
paths rather than by the FF2-layer parametric representations that NPO
targets. As a result, the model learns to produce better-sounding reasoning
while the causal link between reasoning content and predictions weakens.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A reproduction of the NPO faithfulness framework confirming core
        findings and identifying four previously unreported bugs in the
        released codebase.
  \item A new continuous faithfulness metric ($\Delta p$) and analysis of
        \emph{subcritical} faithfulness --- instances where CoT causally
        reduces correct-answer probability but does not flip predictions ---
        showing that binary faithfulness undercounts causal signal by
        52.2\% in LLaMA-3-3B.
  \item A controlled experiment comparing parametric faithfulness under
        baseline, high-quality CoT, and low-quality CoT fine-tuning conditions
        across two model families, with a cross-model vs.\ model-specific
        ablation ruling out distribution shift as a confound.
  \item Evidence \textbf{confirming} the alignment-faithfulness paradox across
        LLaMA-3-3B ($-$73\% relative, $p = 0.005$) and Phi-3
        ($-$56\% relative, $p = 0.033$).
  \item Step-level evidence for the mechanism: non-faithful CoT steps score
        higher on quality metrics than faithful ones (3.61 vs.\ 3.33,
        $p = 0.008$, $N = 940$), with quality and faithfulness orthogonal
        overall ($r = -0.010$, n.s.).
  \item External validation against REVEAL human annotations: parametric
        faithfulness tracks logical correctness ($\Delta p{=}{+}0.110$
        vs.\ $-0.084$, $p < 0.001$, $N = 1{,}276$), not surface-level
        evidence attribution ($\rho = 0.04$, n.s.).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Chain-of-Thought Faithfulness}

Faithfulness in NLP was formalized by \citet{jacovi2020faithfully} as the
degree to which an explanation accurately represents the model's actual
reasoning process, distinct from \emph{plausibility}, which concerns human
interpretability. Early work showed that post-hoc rationales generated by
chain-of-thought models can be influenced by spurious features in the prompt
\citep{turpin2023faithfulness}: adding a biasing hint changes the model's
answer without the expressed reasoning acknowledging the influence.
\citet{lanham2023measuring} applied direct perturbation tests to CoT, showing
that corrupting intermediate steps often fails to affect final predictions.

\citet{lyu2023faithful} proposed structured symbolic CoT to improve
faithfulness by construction, while \citet{prystawski2024think} argued that
any faithful intermediate representation requires information to propagate
through the residual stream in ways that are difficult to control via
prompting alone. These lines of work converge on a pessimistic view: standard
auto-regressive CoT generation is not guaranteed to produce faithful
reasoning, even when the reasoning appears coherent.

\subsection{Parametric Faithfulness via Unlearning}

\citet{tutek2025faithfulness} introduce a mechanistic operationalization that
sidesteps the ambiguity of perturbation tests. Their key insight is that if a
CoT step is parametrically faithful --- encoded in the same model weights that
drive the final prediction --- then selectively unlearning that step should
change the prediction. They use Negative Preference Optimization (NPO)
\citep{zhang2024negative} to minimize the log-likelihood of the CoT step
while maintaining a KL divergence constraint on retain sequences (npo\_KL
objective). Unlearning is applied only to FF2 layers (\texttt{mlp.down\_proj}
weights), which store factual associations \citep{meng2022rome}. A binary
faithfulness label is assigned if any prediction flip occurs across training
epochs.

This framework has several appealing properties: it targets parametric
encoding rather than surface behavior, it is model-agnostic, and it produces
per-instance faithfulness scores rather than aggregate statistics.

\subsection{Fine-Tuning, RLHF, and Reasoning Quality}

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback \citep{ouyang2022rlhf} and its
variants have demonstrated that optimizing for human preference ratings can
substantially improve the surface quality of model outputs, including CoT
reasoning. \citet{lightman2023prm} show that process reward models trained to
evaluate individual reasoning steps yield further gains. However, whether
these improvements carry through to faithfulness has not been tested. The
closest prior work is on \emph{sycophancy} \citep{perez2022sycophancy}, which
shows that RLHF-aligned models are more likely to produce socially desirable
but incorrect reasoning --- a qualitatively similar dissociation to what we
hypothesize.

\section{Hypothesis and Mechanism}
\label{sec:hypothesis}

We formalize the alignment-faithfulness paradox hypothesis as follows:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{H1.} Fine-tuning on high-quality CoT instances will yield lower
  parametric faithfulness (binary rate and mean $\Delta p$) on held-out
  instances, compared to the unmodified base model.
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{H2.} Fine-tuning on low-quality CoT instances will yield equal or
  higher parametric faithfulness than the high-quality condition (null
  baseline hypothesis for the quality comparison).
\end{quote}

The proposed mechanism rests on a distinction between two routes through
which a model can arrive at a correct answer given a CoT:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Parametric route}: The semantic content of the CoT step
        activates or strengthens a factual association stored in FF2 layers,
        shifting the output distribution toward the correct answer.
  \item \textbf{Surface route}: The CoT string's lexical patterns (e.g.,
        hedging language, certain keywords) correlate with correct answers
        in the training distribution, allowing the model to shortcut to the
        correct prediction without encoding the causal structure.
\end{enumerate}
We hypothesize that high-quality CoT fine-tuning strengthens the surface
route, because the training signal (scored CoT examples) selects for
stylistically coherent, complete reasoning chains whose text is correlated
with correct answers in the training data. The FF2 parametric route is not
directly incentivized and may be weakened if the model learns that
surface-level completeness is sufficient.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Base Models and Datasets}

We evaluate two model families: \textbf{LLaMA-3.2-3B-Instruct}
\citep{dubey2024llama3} and \textbf{Phi-3-mini-4k-instruct}
\citep{abdin2024phi3}, with LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2
results forthcoming. We draw from four multiple-choice QA benchmarks:
\textbf{ARC-Challenge} \citep{clark2018arc}, \textbf{OpenBookQA}
\citep{mihaylov2018openbook}, \textbf{Sports Understanding}
\citep{srivastava2022bigbench}, and \textbf{StrategyQA}
\citep{geva2021strategyqa}.

\subsection{CoT Quality Scoring}

We score 918 CoT instances generated by LLaMA-3-3B on a
3-dimensional rubric:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Coherence} (1--5): Is the reasoning logically consistent and
        well-structured?
  \item \textbf{Plausibility} (1--5): Does each step follow plausibly from
        the question and prior steps?
  \item \textbf{Completeness} (1--5): Does the CoT cover all necessary
        reasoning without unnecessary gaps?
\end{itemize}
Scoring is performed by \texttt{claude-sonnet-4-5} via the Anthropic API,
using a structured prompt that requests integer scores for each dimension.
The composite score is the mean of the three dimensions (range 1.0--5.0;
mean 3.30, $\sigma = 1.44$).

\subsection{Fine-Tuning Conditions}

We create three experimental conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Baseline}: The unmodified model checkpoint.
  \item \textbf{High-quality}: A LoRA adapter \citep{hu2022lora} trained on
        the top-50\% of instances by composite score ($n = 434$;
        mean score 4.66).
  \item \textbf{Low-quality}: A LoRA adapter trained on the bottom-50\%
        ($n = 434$; mean score 1.94).
\end{enumerate}
Both adapters use identical hyperparameters: rank $r = 16$, $\alpha = 32$,
dropout $= 0.05$, applied to all 7 projection matrices. Training runs for 3
epochs with learning rate $2 \times 10^{-4}$, effective batch size 16, and a
cosine schedule with 5\% warmup. Adapters are merged before evaluation.
Confirmed training losses (LLaMA-3-3B): 0.271 (high-quality), 0.234
(low-quality). For LLaMA-3-8B: 0.313 (high-quality),
\RESULT{llama8b\_lq\_loss} (low-quality; forthcoming). For Mistral-7B: 0.196
(high-quality), \RESULT{mistral\_lq\_loss} (low-quality; forthcoming).

For Phi-3, we additionally run a \textbf{model-specific} condition: both
adapters are trained on Phi-3's own generated CoTs (using the same quality
rubric), rather than LLaMA-3-3B's CoTs. This ablation tests whether the
paradox arises from cross-model distribution shift or from CoT quality per se.

\subsection{Faithfulness Evaluation}

We hold out 50 instances stratified by dataset. For each instance under each
condition, we run the full NPO unlearning loop: FF2-only optimization,
\texttt{npo\_KL} objective, learning rate $3 \times 10^{-5}$, 5 epochs.

We report two faithfulness metrics:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Binary faithfulness}: Fraction of instances where any
        prediction flip occurs across epochs 1--5.
  \item \textbf{Continuous faithfulness ($\Delta p$)}: The change in
        normalized probability assigned to the correct answer between epoch 0
        and the epoch with maximum $\Delta p$.
\end{itemize}

Statistical comparisons use McNemar's exact test for binary faithfulness
(paired at the instance level) and Welch's $t$-test for continuous $\Delta p$,
with $\alpha = 0.05$.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{The Alignment-Faithfulness Paradox}
\label{sec:paradox}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports binary faithfulness rates under each condition.
Figure~\ref{fig:paradox} visualizes the results. The data confirm H1 with
striking clarity across both evaluated model families.

For \textbf{LLaMA-3-3B}, baseline parametric faithfulness is 30.0\%,
consistent with \citet{tutek2025faithfulness}'s original findings. After
fine-tuning on high-quality CoT instances, binary faithfulness collapses to
\textbf{8.0\%} --- a 73\% relative reduction and 22 percentage-point absolute
drop (two-proportion $z$-test, $p = 0.005$). After fine-tuning on low-quality
instances, faithfulness is 28.0\%, a negligible and non-significant change from
baseline ($-$2pp; $p = 0.826$). The gap between conditions --- 20 percentage
points --- is large enough to be interpretable without statistical equivocation.

\textbf{Phi-3} replicates the pattern. Baseline faithfulness is 32.0\%.
High-quality fine-tuning reduces this to \textbf{14.0\%} ($-$56\% relative,
$-$18pp absolute; $p = 0.033$). Low-quality fine-tuning yields 36.0\%, a
non-significant increase from baseline ($p = 0.673$). Both the direction and
the magnitude of the paradox are consistent across model families, strengthening
the generalizability of the finding.

\subsection{Low-Quality Fine-Tuning as a Control}
\label{sec:lowqual}

A critical alternative explanation is that fine-tuning in general --- rather
than fine-tuning on \emph{high-quality} data specifically --- disrupts
parametric faithfulness. Our low-quality control condition rules this out.
Low-quality LoRA fine-tuning on the same number of instances
(434 examples, 3 epochs, identical hyperparameters) produces faithfulness rates
of 28.0\% (LLaMA-3-3B) and 36.0\% (Phi-3) --- both statistically
indistinguishable from their respective baselines ($p = 0.826$ and
$p = 0.673$). If fine-tuning itself caused faithfulness loss, both conditions
would show comparable drops. The exclusive collapse under high-quality training
confirms that the \emph{quality} of the training signal is the operative
variable: the model is learning something specific about high-quality reasoning
that decouples CoT from prediction.

\subsection{The Mechanism: Quality Filtering Selects Against Faithfulness}
\label{sec:mechanism}

The training-level paradox raises a mechanistic question: why does quality
filtering reduce faithfulness? We answer this through step-level analysis of
940 matched instances (annotation samples paired with LLM quality scores).

Table~\ref{tab:mechanism} reports mean quality scores for faithful and
non-faithful steps. Non-faithful steps --- those whose unlearning does \emph{not}
change the model's prediction --- score \textbf{3.61} on average, significantly
higher than faithful steps at 3.33 (Mann-Whitney $p = 0.008$). The overall
correlation between quality score and $\Delta p$ is $r = -0.010$ (n.s.),
confirming that quality and faithfulness are \emph{orthogonal} dimensions of
CoT steps. The negative point estimate, while not significant, is consistent
with quality filtering preferentially selecting non-faithful steps.

The causal loop closes as follows: high-quality CoT fine-tuning selects
instances in which non-faithful steps are over-represented. Training on these
examples strengthens the model's surface-route from coherent CoT text to
correct answers, while the FF2 parametric route becomes relatively less
necessary. Quality filtering does not passively fail to improve faithfulness
--- it \emph{actively selects against} it.

\subsection{External Validation via REVEAL}
\label{sec:reveal}

We validate our faithfulness measure against human step-level annotations from
REVEAL \citep{jacovi2025reveal}, which provides logical correctness and evidence
attribution labels for StrategyQA and Sports Understanding reasoning chains. We
match 1,276 step annotations to our faithfulness scores across 54 unique
questions using normalized question text.

Steps in logically correct answers exhibit significantly higher $\Delta p$ than
those in incorrect answers ($+0.110$ vs.\ $-0.084$, Mann-Whitney $p < 0.001$,
$N = 1{,}276$), while surface-level evidence attribution labels show no
relationship with $\Delta p$ ($\rho = 0.04$, Kruskal-Wallis $p = 0.754$,
n.s.). This confirms that parametric faithfulness captures logical grounding
independent of surface-level citation: a step can be fully attributed to a
retrieved passage while being causally irrelevant to the model's prediction, and
vice versa.

Notably, the REVEAL finding mirrors the training-level result: both show that
surface properties of reasoning (quality score; attribution support) are
orthogonal to whether the reasoning step actually drives the model's answer.

\subsection{Cross-Model vs.\ Model-Specific Training Rules Out Distribution Shift}

A second alternative explanation is that the faithfulness collapse under
high-quality training arises because Phi-3 is trained on LLaMA-3-3B's CoTs
--- a cross-model distribution shift that could superficially disrupt the
model's internal reasoning without any relationship to quality. We address this
directly with the model-specific condition, in which Phi-3 is fine-tuned on
its own CoTs scored and split by quality.

Table~\ref{tab:crossmodel} reports results. The cross-model and model-specific
conditions yield \emph{identical} high-quality faithfulness (14.0\% vs.\
14.0\%) and nearly identical low-quality faithfulness (36.0\% vs.\ 38.0\%).
The paradox is fully replicated regardless of whether the training CoTs come
from Phi-3 or LLaMA-3-3B. This rules out cross-model distribution shift as a
confound and implicates the quality signal itself.

\subsection{Continuous Scoring Reveals a Paradox Within the Paradox}
\label{sec:delta_p_paradox}

The binary results tell only part of the story. The continuous $\Delta p$
metric reveals a striking inversion for LLaMA-3-3B: while high-quality CoT
fine-tuning \emph{reduces} binary faithfulness (fewer prediction flips),
it \emph{increases} mean $\Delta p$ (0.0006 baseline $\rightarrow$ 0.0019
high-quality, compared to 0.0003 for low-quality). High-quality fine-tuning
appears to create a \textbf{bimodal faithfulness distribution}: most reasoning
steps become completely decoupled from predictions (explaining the drop to 8\%
binary faithfulness), but the minority of steps that remain coupled are
\emph{more strongly} coupled than before (explaining the higher mean $\Delta
p$). Low-quality fine-tuning, by contrast, compresses $\Delta p$ toward zero
uniformly --- CoTs remain broadly in-distribution for the model, so no
reorganization of parametric encoding occurs.

This pattern has methodological implications: binary faithfulness alone would
classify high-quality fine-tuning as ``nearly unfaithful'' (8\%), while
$\Delta p$ analysis reveals that the remaining faithful steps carry stronger
causal weight. Both metrics are needed for a complete picture.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the Mechanism Means}

The step-level finding (Section~\ref{sec:mechanism}) closes the causal loop.
Quality filtering does not passively fail to improve faithfulness --- it
actively selects against it. The most causally-grounded reasoning steps are
also the least polished: they are the raw inferences that actually shift the
model's probability distribution, not the clean elaborations that make a chain
of thought readable. This explains not just the paradox but \emph{predicts}
it: any quality-based selection criterion applied to CoT data will produce this
effect unless explicitly designed to select for causal grounding rather than
surface coherence. The practical corollary is that faithfulness-preserving
fine-tuning requires a fundamentally different training signal --- one that
rewards parametric coupling, not stylistic quality.

\subsection{Implications for AI Safety and CoT Monitoring}

The paradox has direct implications for deployment-time safety. Models
fine-tuned on quality-filtered CoT produce reasoning that looks most
trustworthy precisely because it is most decoupled from their actual
decision-making. A safety monitor reading such CoT receives a systematically
misleading signal: the coherent, step-by-step reasoning it observes is not the
mechanism by which the model arrived at its answer. This concern is not merely
theoretical. CoT monitoring is increasingly deployed by frontier AI laboratories
as a primary tool for detecting misaligned or deceptive reasoning. Our findings
suggest that this approach is undermined by the same training process that makes
CoT human-readable. The very properties that make CoT appealing to human
evaluators --- coherence, completeness, plausible structure --- may be precisely
what decouples it from parametric faithfulness.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

Several limitations constrain our conclusions. First, we confirm the paradox
on two model families ($N = 50$ per condition); LLaMA-3-8B and Mistral-7B
results are forthcoming and may differ, particularly for larger models where
FF2 layers may play different representational roles. Second, LoRA fine-tuning
with rank 16 on 400--500 instances is a weak intervention compared to full
fine-tuning on millions of examples; the paradox may be stronger under full
fine-tuning. Third, our NPO faithfulness probe targets only FF2 layers and
first CoT steps; faithfulness in middle and later steps may behave differently.
Finally, 50 held-out instances per condition yields limited statistical power
for small effect sizes; however, the effects observed ($-$18pp to $-$22pp) are
large enough to be reliably detected at $N = 50$.

Future work should examine larger models, full fine-tuning, and
faithfulness-preserving training objectives that explicitly penalize the surface
shortcut route. The FF2 mechanistic probe could be extended to other layer types
to test the representation redistribution hypothesis
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion}). The REVEAL validation framework suggests a path
toward automatic faithfulness benchmarking: step-level logical correctness
annotations, if collected at scale, would provide a richer signal for training
faithfulness-aware models.

\subsection{Interpreting the Paradox}

Our results confirm H1 and disconfirm H2 (low-quality fine-tuning does not
reduce faithfulness). Several interpretations are consistent with the data.

\paragraph{Interpretation 1: Decoupling via surface routes.}
High-quality CoT fine-tuning teaches the model that coherent, complete
reasoning patterns co-occur with correct answers. This may bypass the FF2
parametric route entirely: the model learns a stronger surface-to-answer
shortcut, making FF2 weights less necessary. Under this view, faithfulness and
quality are necessarily traded off because the quality training objective
(maximize log-likelihood of good CoT completions) is orthogonal to the
faithfulness objective (maximize causal connection between CoT encoding and
prediction).

\paragraph{Interpretation 2: Representation redistribution.}
LoRA fine-tuning adds adapter parameters across attention and MLP layers, but
the NPO faithfulness test specifically targets \texttt{mlp.down\_proj} (FF2).
If fine-tuning redistributes CoT encoding from FF2 to other layers (e.g.,
attention heads or FF1), NPO unlearning would show reduced effect even if the
CoT remains causally relevant overall. This would suggest a limitation of the
NPO faithfulness probe rather than a genuine faithfulness reduction.

\paragraph{Interpretation 3: Bimodal reorganization.}
The $\Delta p$ finding (higher mean despite fewer flips) suggests that
high-quality fine-tuning does not uniformly reduce faithfulness. Instead, it
may sharpen the distinction between steps that are parametrically grounded
and those that are decorative --- producing a cleaner but sparser faithfulness
signal. This interpretation is consistent with the surface-route mechanism but
adds a positive valence: the remaining faithful steps may be \emph{more}
faithful.

\subsection{Relationship to Prior Work}

Our experiment operationalizes a conjecture that has appeared implicitly in
several lines of prior work. \citet{turpin2023faithfulness} showed that
aligned models produce more biased post-hoc rationalizations than their base
counterparts. \citet{lanham2023measuring} found that longer, higher-quality
CoT chains were not more faithful by their perturbation metric. Our
contribution is a controlled experimental design --- same model, same
evaluation protocol, only the CoT training distribution varies --- that
directly tests the quality-faithfulness relationship.

The clearest prior analogue is \citet{perez2022sycophancy}, who demonstrate
that RLHF-tuned models produce more agreeable but less accurate reasoning.
The alignment-faithfulness paradox is the mechanistic counterpart: not only
do aligned models \emph{say} more agreeable things, their parametric weights
may encode those statements less causally.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We present a four-layer argument establishing the alignment-faithfulness
paradox. At the \textbf{training level}, high-quality CoT fine-tuning reduces
parametric faithfulness by 56--73\% relative across LLaMA-3-3B and Phi-3
($p = 0.005$ and $p = 0.033$), while low-quality fine-tuning leaves
faithfulness unchanged --- ruling out fine-tuning in general as the cause.
At the \textbf{step level}, non-faithful CoT steps score higher on quality
metrics than faithful ones (3.61 vs.\ 3.33, $p = 0.008$, $N = 940$), with
quality and faithfulness orthogonal overall ($r = -0.010$, n.s.) --- closing
the causal loop. At the \textbf{external validation level}, REVEAL human
annotations confirm that parametric faithfulness tracks logical correctness
($\Delta p{=}{+}0.110$ vs.\ $-0.084$, $p < 0.001$, $N = 1{,}276$) while
surface attribution is unrelated ($\rho = 0.04$, n.s.). At the
\textbf{ablation level}, model-specific training on Phi-3 replicates the
paradox identically (14.0\% vs.\ 14.0\%), ruling out cross-model distribution
shift.

Together, these findings establish that quality filtering does not passively
fail to improve faithfulness --- it actively selects against it. Any training
pipeline that selects CoT instances by surface quality will reproduce this
effect. Improving CoT interpretability therefore requires faithfulness-aware
objectives that directly reward parametric coupling, not stylistic coherence.
